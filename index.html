The House committee’s report on antisemitism, annotated

By Esha Karam, Ayaan Ali. Graphics by Ayaan Ali

November 22, 2024

Zohar Ford / Staff Photographer

The House Committee on Education and the Workforce published a 325-page report on antisemitism on college campuses on Oct. 31, the culmination of over 11 months of scrutiny of 11 universities. The Republican-led committee used its subpoena powers over two universities—Columbia and Harvard—for the first time in its history.

Over 143 pages—nearly half of the report—centers on the committee’s investigation into Columbia and Barnard, publishing information from the over 50,000 pages of confidential documents that the University submitted following the August subpoena. Columbia was the only university to be referenced in each of the report’s four key conclusions.

Spectator annotated the pages of the report that include details about Columbia and Barnard. Here are Spectator’s top five takeaways:

“Antisemitism on College Campuses Exposed,” annotated

Key Takeaways

1

Internal documents disclose previously unknown details about the negotiations between Columbia University Apartheid Divest organizers and University administrators. Trustees played a large role in negotiations, and several deans and the chair of the University Senate executive committee served as liaisons between students and administrators. Documents show that Columbia was prepared to make several concessions to comply with protesters’ requests, but that CUAD’s negotiators wanted more than what the University was willing to offer.

2

Correspondence between then-University President Minouche Shafik and members of Columbia’s board of trustees show that trustees had reservations about cooperating with House Republicans’ investigations. Board of trustees co-chair Claire Shipman, CC ’86, SIPA ’94, described the congressional investigation as “capital hill nonsense.” Several trustees wrote that they hoped Democrats would win control of the House in the 2024 election under the assumption that congressional scrutiny would ease. Shafik wrote that she met with Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer, who advised her that Columbia’s “political problems are really only among Republicans.”

Internal communications reveal that top administrators and trustees had concerns about biases among members of the University Senate. One trustee referred to the “antisemites on the Senate” and suggested that the trustees should hold a vote of no confidence in the Senate. Journalism School Dean Jelani Cobb and Columbia College Dean Josef Sorett also expressed concerns about the body. “Senate sounds like CUAD,” Sorett wrote in one text message. “It’s a spinoff,” Cobb replied.

3

4

The appendices of the report contain dozens of pages detailing hundreds of disciplinary proceedings at Columbia and Barnard after Oct. 7, 2023, including some related to protest activity and others not related to demonstrations. The records show that, despite publicly committing to suspending or expelling students who violated University policy, Columbia was much more lenient in implementing sanctions.

5

Many documents that Columbia submitted are included in the report and marked “confidential.” A University spokesperson told Spectator that the University “did not consent” to the publication of the documents and that the committee provided Columbia with one day’s notice before publishing the report. A Barnard spokesperson also confirmed that the college did not consent to the publication of the materials.

Shafik testified in front of the Committee on April 17 after a scheduling conflict prevented her from attending the December 2023 hearing. She testified alongside board of trustees co-chairs Shipman and David Greenwald, Law ’83, as well as David Schizer, dean emeritus of the Law School and co-chair of the Columbia Task Force on Antisemitism.

The committee issued two subpoenas: one to Harvard on Feb. 16 and one to Columbia on Aug. 21.

A quarter of the 400,000 pages came from Columbia, according to a committee spokesperson.

A large focus of the report is the extent of disciplinary action imposed in response to alleged antisemitic incidents and on-campus demonstrations, including the April 30 Hamilton Hall occupation.

Columbia is the only university listed under each of the four “key findings” in the summary of the report.

The report includes internal communications between students and administrative negotiators during the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” that reveal previously unknown details about what Shafik and Columbia trustees offered protesters. See Pages 18 through 23 of the report for this section.

The report discusses a Jan. 19 incident in which pro-Palestinian protesters reported being sprayed with an odorous substance. It states that the University failed to issue statements after discovering through its investigation that the chemical was a commercially available non-toxic spray. See Pages 48 through 56 of the report for this section.

The report discusses the University Senate’s push to move disciplinary processes from the Center for Student Success and Innovation to the University Judicial Board, which, among other protections, grants students the right to an attorney. The report says that the senate expressed preference toward the student protesters and includes messages between administrators discussing the senate’s bias. See Pages 88 through 106 of the report for this section.

The committee took issue with messages between Shafik and members of the board of trustees included in the report that suggested they viewed the committee’s scrutiny of Columbia as a Republican issue. See Pages 115 through 118 of the report for this section.

The committee exercised its authority to investigate universities’ obligations under Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. The Department of Education is also responsible for monitoring the Title VI compliance of universities that receive federal funding and is currently investigating Columbia for multiple potential violations of Title VI.

Columbia has also faced lawsuits alleging that the University has violated Title VI by creating a hostile environment for Jewish students.

Protests and events on Columbia’s campus have ignited debates over universities’ responsibilities to protect academic freedom and freedom of speech and, at the same time, curb antisemitism.

In a message to the Columbia community on April 29, Shafik announced that negotiations between “academic leaders” and student organizers had stalled. “Both sides in these discussions put forward robust and thoughtful offers and worked in good faith to reach common ground,” she wrote. The committee’s report claims that the concessions that Columbia offered “rewarded radical students.”

One document that Columbia provided to the committee revealed a full list of student organizers’ demands, many of which were shared publicly by the organizers during the encampment.

Shafik wrote in her April 29 message that the University “offered to convene a faculty committee to address academic freedom” in response to this demand.

The committee criticized Columbia for engaging in negotiations with student protesters and responding to their demands.

Shafik had written publicly that “While the University will not divest from Israel, the University offered to develop an expedited timeline for review of new proposals from the students by the Advisory Committee for Socially Responsible Investing.” The documents included in the report reveal that the University was considering a timeline of six months for reviewing proposals of divesting from specific companies tied to Israel or the manufacturing of weapons.

In her April 29 communication, Shafik wrote that the University offered to “begin a discussion on access and financial barriers to academic programs and global centers.” This document reveals that the University considered discussing access to the Tel Aviv University dual degree program and the Tel Aviv Global Center with the Israeli government. It also disclosed that the University was looking to partner with two universities: Al Quds University, located in the West Bank, and Haifa University, located in northwest Israel.

The University considered offering amnesty for students who participated in the first “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” on April 18 and had no prior offenses. Over 100 individuals were arrested by the New York Police Department on April 18 for their participation in the encampment. Shafik did not mention amnesty for student protesters in her April 29 message.

The “menu” of options shows that the University considered outlining a process for reinstating Columbia’s chapters of Students for Justice in Palestine and Jewish Voice for Peace. Then-Senior Executive Vice President Gerald Rosberg said that the policies that Columbia cited in SJP and JVP’s suspensions were altered without University Senate input weeks prior to the groups’ suspensions. At a Nov. 17, 2023 University Senate plenary, Rosberg committed to reexamining the policies and said, “I know I am going to be living with this for a long time to come.”

Shafik wrote in her April 29 message that “the University also offered to make investments in health and education in Gaza, including supporting early childhood development and support for displaced scholars.”

New documents reveal the specific details of the investments that Columbia offered to make. In September, interim University President Katrina Armstrong announced that Columbia was establishing a “University International Student Hardship Fund,” through which students from regions experiencing “natural disasters, armed conflict, humanitarian crises, and other circumstances” can apply for grants.

Student organizers with CUAD countered the University’s concessions with more demands, including asking the University to commit at least $10 million to a proposed resilience fund for Gaza and requiring the scholarship fund to cover 30 to 40 students for five years.

The report’s appendix includes the draft of a statement written by student organizers that they wanted Shafik to send to the Columbia community. The draft statement describes the war in Gaza as “plausibly a genocide” and states that “Palestinians are surviving a myriad of other injustices.” It continues to call the University’s disciplinary processes “overly punitive and viewpoint discriminatory.” The draft email also says that NYPD presence on campus “has contributed to a sense of danger” and describes a “McCarthyist censorship of our faculty.”

 

The appendix includes emails between then-Executive Vice President for Public Affairs Shailagh Murray and Shafik in response to the draft statement. Shafik replied to an email with the draft statement attached, calling it “unacceptable.” Murray responded that she was “really uncomfortable giving them sign off on anything from you” and that “It’s too easy a target if they are looking for an excuse to blow this up.”

Spectator reported that nearly two dozen students experienced a foul smell, physical symptoms, or property damage at a Jan. 19 protest.

According to emails quoted in the report’s footnotes, four administrators who were present at the protest did not notice any foul smell. Gerald Lewis, vice president of public safety, wrote that “to my knowledge this is untrue,” and that “We also have not had any reports of such actions from our personnel,” the footnote states.

In an email to the community shortly after the protest, then-interim University Provost Dennis Mitchell wrote that the University had been “actively working with local and federal authorities.” Mitchell described the incident as “serious crimes, potentially hate crimes.”

Layla Saliba, SSW ’25, a student who attended the Jan. 19 protest, told Spectator that she sought medical attention at an urgent care after the protest. Two other students told Spectator that they sought medical care.

According to the disciplinary records the University provided to the committee, the student perpetrators—who were initially banned from campus following the protest—produced Amazon receipts showing that they purchased a commercially available “fart spray.” On April 16, one of the students filed a lawsuit against the University, claiming that Columbia pursued “biased misconduct proceedings” and that the student’s “actions were a harmless expression of speech.”

After the Jan. 19 protest, student protesters described the chemical spray as “Skunk spray,” a chemical the Israeli military has used against protesters in the West Bank.

Brian Cohen, executive director of Columbia/Barnard Hillel, and a Jewish student leader asked Shafik in separate instances in March and April to update the community on the investigation into the incident and clarify the nature of the chemicals used.

The committee cited in a footnote a “Confidential Settlement Agreement” as proof the University reached a $395,000 settlement with the student perpetrator who filed a lawsuit. The University decreased the student’s year-and-a-half suspension to conditional disciplinary probation.

The committee highlighted Shafik’s decision not to issue a statement in support of Columbia students who are part of its dual degree program between Tel Aviv University and the School of General Studies, despite several top administrators calling on her to do so.

Last spring, students in Columbia College and Barnard passed referendums through their school councils to cancel the dual degree program. Students in the School of Engineering and Applied Science also voted in favor of ending the dual degree program in a survey sent out by the Engineering Student Council this fall.

After learning of the Columbia College Student Council’s referendum on canceling the dual degree program, General Studies Dean Lisa Rosen-Metsch wrote an April 12 email to Shafik asking her to consider issuing a public statement in support of the program. Cohen sent an email the same day expressing a similar sentiment.

Murray wrote in an April 12 email that “Brian is never happy!” in reference to Cohen’s concerns. Chief Operating Officer Cas Holloway replied that he agreed that Shafik should release a statement in support of the dual degree program and its students. “This isn’t about Brian,” he wrote. “It’s just wrong.”

CSSI refers to the Center for Student Success and Intervention, an administrative unit established in 2022. It consists of three subdivisions: student conduct, case management, and a student support specialist. Transferring the cases from CSSI to the University Judicial Board, a five-member body separate from the senate’s Rules of University Conduct committee, implements the “Rules process” as the procedure for each case. Unlike the CSSI process, the Rules process grants students the right to an attorney and the ability to call witnesses and advisors to assist with their case.

The report argues that administrative oversight of the Rules process was biased due to some faculty members on the Rules of University Conduct committee having expressed support for the encampment and related pro-Palestinian activism. The committee reviews and revises the Rules, whereas the University Judicial Board adjudicates Rules violations.

CSSI refers to the Center for Student Success and Intervention, an administrative unit established in 2022. It consists of three subdivisions: student conduct, case management, and a student support specialist. Transferring the cases from CSSI to the University Judicial Board, a five-member body separate from the senate’s Rules of University Conduct committee, implements the “Rules process” as the procedure for each case. The report argues that administrative oversight of the Rules process was biased due to some faculty members on the Rules of University Conduct committee having expressed support for the encampment and related pro-Palestinian activism. The committee reviews and revises the Rules, whereas the University Judicial Board adjudicates Rules violations.

The report identifies faculty members Joseph Slaughter, professor of English and comparative literature, and Susan Bernofsky, professor of writing, as having a potential conflict of interest between their support for the encampment and their membership in the Rules Committee of the University Senate. Bernofsky and Slaughter were seen wearing orange vests that demarcated faculty members near the encampment on West Lawn, according to photos published in the Washington Free Beacon. At an Aug. 23 University Senate plenary, chair of the University Senate’s executive committee Jeanine D’Armiento said that faculty action was coordinated in collaboration with Holloway and Lewis. The faculty were there to “prevent any escalation of activities,” D’Armiento said.

The Rules Committee is responsible for reviewing and revising the Rules of University Conduct, which the University Judicial Board uses to decide cases during initiated Rules processes. Every four years, the Committee revises and reviews the Rules of University Conduct. In the “Guidelines to the Rules of University Conduct,” endorsed by the University Senate on Aug. 23, the Rules committee underscores that one of its central goals is to “protect freedom of expression.” Neither Slaughter nor Bernofsky are Rules administrators or members of the UJB, which was responsible for reviewing cases pertaining to the Hamilton occupation.

Text messages between Shafik and Mitchell show that the two discussed continuing dialogue with student protesters, both in terms of their demands during the encampment and engaging them in revising the Rules of University Conduct.

According to the report, the conversation between Shafik, Shipman, and Greenwald demonstrates a “substantial weakening” of the administration’s position on student discipline. Shafik suggested using the more restrictive CSSI process for seven “repeat offenders,” and disciplining those who occupied Hamilton through the UJB procedure. Shipman and Greenwald warned that this would not meet the expectations of the board of trustees.

Jonathan Lavine, chair emeritus of the board of trustees, suggested that the trustees take a vote of no confidence on senate and faculty leadership. The report suggests that this is a culmination of “shock and disdain” for the University Senate’s “radicalism.”

An exchange between Sorett and Cobb is used as evidence to demonstrate an overarching sentiment toward the University Senate.

Michael Nutter is the David N. Dinkins professor of professional practice in urban and public affairs. According to Shipman, Nutter described the conversations in University Senate plenaries as indicative of a “big political crisis.” Their conversations lead into how to “activate other faculty” that are, according to Nutter, less “nuts.”

The report puts considerable emphasis on Jeanine D’Armiento, chair of the executive committee of the University Senate, labeling her actions as “hypocritical” of a “purported commitment to freedom of expression.” D’Armiento served as a key liaison between student negotiators and administrators during the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment.” In October, a petition circulated expressing concerns that the University Senate might reelect D’Armiento to a fourth term as executive committee chair.

Shafik was invited to testify before the Committee, chaired by Virginia Foxx (R-NC), on Dec. 5, 2023, but could not attend due to a scheduling conflict. The article Shipman refers to was written by New York Times reporter Sharon Otterman and detailed how Shafik avoided media backlash by not attending the hearing. Later, Shafik was invited to testify in front of the same committee alongside Shipman, Greenwald, and Schizer.

The report argues that both Shafik’s communication with Schumer along with Lavine’s message about the upcoming midterm elections indicated that University administrators saw the hearing as partisan and “hoped to escape accountability.”

Congress does not have the authority to find universities in violation of Title VI: Only the Department of Education’s Office of Civil Rights can come to such a conclusion and administer consequences.

With Republicans securing control of the House of Representatives, the Senate, and the White House, it is likely that pressure on Columbia and its peers will continue.

The University did not agree to issue any presidential statement drafted by student organizers, such as the one provided on Page 283.

It is unclear what iteration of the administrators’ menu of responses this document represents.

In this document, dated April 27, CUAD submits counter-proposals to the concessions that Columbia offered to student organizers.

Student organizers pushed for greater financial transparency than the University initially offered—they asked for access to all of Columbia’s indirect holdings, including exchange-traded funds, private equity funds, and hedge funds.

CUAD organizers and administrators agreed that Shafik would issue a statement in support of academic freedom and establish a faculty committee on the topic.

Student organizers requested full, unconditional amnesty and did not want to sign attestations stating that they must comply with University policies until December 2024.

The University did not agree to issue any presidential statement drafted by student organizers, such as the one provided on Page 283.

The University requested that protesters dismantle the encampment within six hours of a final negotiation; CUAD requested 24 hours. CUAD also did not agree to the University’s request to not establish another encampment, writing that the decentralized nature of the coalition meant it was “beyond the control of CUAD.”
